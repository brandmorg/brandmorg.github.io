Throughout The Lord of the Rings, there are many hints that Higher Powers are involved. These hints become even more clear to those who have read The Silmarillion, and understand the relationship between the Valar and Middle-earth, such as the association of Manwe and the wind and Great Eagles, or Varda and the stars, or Ulmo and the rivers and streams.


Pay close attention to the weather — winds, fog, stars, clouds, rain — and you will see that it signals a battle between Sauron and the Valar to which the people of Middle-earth are, for the most part, oblivious. As for Eru, his intervention is even more subtle, but, as Gandalf says, Someone wanted Bilbo to find the Ring, and Someone sent Gandalf back from death stronger than ever.

Saruman's assertion of "many colors" is thematically consistent with Gandalf's remarks about how a man who breaks a thing to find out what it is has left the path of wisdom. White light technically does contain many colors, but they're only visible when the light is "broken" by a prism. Saruman broke himself by aspiring for more power than he was meant to wield.

The Prophecy of the Witch-King specifcally mentions Man, which everyone knows because the whole 'woman kills him' thing, but Gandalf was absolutely the one that was going to kill him, and Theoden and Eowyn would have lived, if not for the madness of Denethor. It's also possible that Elrond foresaw the possibility of needing to kill the Witch-King, and that is why he wanted to send Glorfindel (again, not a Man, also imbued with incredible powers close to that of the Istari when he was sent back).

Theories abound that the ring represented the nuclear bomb or that Mordor represented Nazi Germany. Published as it was, shortly after World War II, one can see the attraction of this type of thinking. And Tolkien was a public critic of certain aspects of the war, including the bombing of Japan. But Tolkien began writing The Lord of the Rings in 1937, long before the bomb came out and he has gone on record over and over in stating that the story was not an allegory. Tolkien once wrote that:


“…real war does not resemble the legendary war in its process or its conclusion. If it had inspired or directed the development of the legend, then certainly the Ring would have been seized and used against Sauron; he would not have been annihilated but enslaved, and Barad-Dûr would not have been destroyed but occupied. Saruman, failing to get possession of the Ring, would in the confusion and treacheries of the time have found in Mordor the missing links in his own researches into Ring-lore, and before long he would have made a Great Ring of his own with which to challenge the self-styled Ruler of Middle-earth. In that conflict both sides would have held hobbits in hatred and contempt: they would not long have survived even as slaves.”
